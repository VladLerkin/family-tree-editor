# Example: DNA to Genealogy Mapping

How to connect consumer DNA test results to documented ancestor lines.

## Starting Data

The researcher has:
- 23andMe results: 48% Scandinavian, 22% Eastern European, 13% French/German, 8% Ashkenazi Jewish, 3% Finnish, 6% Broadly European
- MyHeritage results: 47% Scandinavian, 12% Baltic, 28% North/West European, 9% Ashkenazi Jewish, 4% Other
- Documented tree: 3 Scandinavian grandparent lines, 1 Eastern European grandparent line

## Step 1: Identify Strong Signals

**~48% Scandinavian**: Both providers agree (48% vs 47%). Three documented grandparent lines are Scandinavian. This is a Strong Signal. The proportion is consistent with 3 of 4 grandparent lines (expected ~75% if all three were 100% Scandinavian, but it is lower because some Scandinavian lines may carry other ancestry and inheritance is not exactly 25% per grandparent).

Wait: 48% for 3 Scandinavian lines is lower than expected. This suggests either (a) some Scandinavian lines carry non-Scandinavian ancestry, or (b) one "Scandinavian" line is actually from a non-Scandinavian country (this turned out to be the case: one "Norwegian" grandparent was actually Swedish, which may be categorized differently by some algorithms).

**~8 to 9% Ashkenazi Jewish**: Both providers agree (8.3% vs 8.6%). Ashkenazi is very genetically distinctive. This is real, not noise. But NO ancestor in the tree is documented as Jewish. This opens an investigation: which ancestor line carries this signal?

## Step 2: Apply the Assignment Framework

For the Ashkenazi signal:
1. **Does genealogical evidence predict it?** Not directly. But one grandparent line traces to Vienna, where Jewish-Christian intermarriage was common in the 19th century. A collateral relative married into a documented Jewish family.
2. **Is the magnitude plausible?** 8% is consistent with one great-great-grandparent being fully Ashkenazi (expected ~6.25%) or partial Ashkenazi ancestry across multiple lines.
3. **Are there alternatives?** The three Scandinavian lines are unlikely sources of Ashkenazi DNA. The Vienna line is the strongest candidate.
4. **Can phasing confirm?** Without a parent's test, assignment is probabilistic. But: the X chromosome is 100% Scandinavian (the researcher is male, so X comes from mother). This means ALL Ashkenazi ancestry is paternal. The Vienna line IS on the paternal side. Consistent.

**Conclusion**: Ashkenazi signal almost certainly enters through the Vienna line. Specific ancestor not yet identified but narrowed to 2 to 3 candidates.

## Step 3: Address Moderate Signals

**~13 to 28% French/German**: Wide range between providers. The Vienna ancestors were German-speaking Austrians, which is genetically similar to German and French populations. This is a Moderate Signal: the ancestry is real, but the exact percentage is uncertain because Austrian, German, and French populations overlap genetically.

**~3% Finnish**: No known Finnish ancestor. Both providers detect it (3.1% vs 2.2%). Likely ancient Finno-Ugric admixture common in Scandinavian populations. Not worth investigating genealogically.

## Lessons

1. DNA confirms broad ancestry composition but cannot pinpoint specific ancestors without additional evidence
2. The X chromosome (for males) is a powerful tool for separating maternal from paternal ancestry
3. Provider disagreements usually reflect different categorization, not different DNA
4. Small percentages (<5%) should not drive research decisions
5. The most interesting finding (Ashkenazi signal) required genealogical investigation to interpret
